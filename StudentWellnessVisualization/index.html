What Stresses Us Out +
How We Deal With Them?

visualized data result from co-design

A design research and visualization project by Yvette Shen, Dr. Elizabeth Sanders, Anne Knellinger, Deana Sagaris, and Kaixin Luo

We are exploring the problems students face in their everyday lives that elicit stress and anxiety, as well as their experiences and strategies of dealing with these problems. We are also looking into the mental health support that is currently offered to students on the Ohio State University(OSU)'s campus

We will use the result of this research project to design a supportive community that promotes positive thinking and positive behavaiors in the future. We will do so by co-deisgning with OSU students and mental health providers. We call this project Design for the Pursuit of Happiness.

What Is Codesign?

Co-design brings the people who will be served by design directly into the prcess as participants. In Design for the Pursuit of Happiness,OSU students and mental health providers whill be he co-designers. Co-design acknowledges that everyone who is affected by (the students) or who uses (the providers) what is designed should play a central role in the design process.

We conducted three co-design sessions with a total of 23 participants. Participants are undergraduate studentd of different levels (freshmen to seniors) and majors at The Ohio State University(Figure top). Then we organize and analyze the different types of data from co-design in order to identify the emerging patterns(Figure bottom).

In a co-design process, designers create different toolkits for non-designers to use to express themselves creatively. in our co-design session with students, we designed toolkits based on quantifiable charts and diagrams for the purpose of answering these research questions.

What are the patterns of students' daliy stress experience?

Based on the data results completed in the Daily Wellbeing Journey toolkit (Toolkit A), the research team identified different patterns of the ups and downs of the stress level in a day. These patterns lead to four types of persona as described in the graph below.Personas are fictional characters created based on research data that represent different user types in a design process. It helps designers to recognize that different people have different needs and expectations.

What are the stress levels students experienced with different academic components?

Based on the completed Toolkit B - Students’ Academic Stress, the graph below displays students’ responses to various academic stressors. We provided the following components: presentations, homework, projects, quizzes, mid-terms/finals in the toolkit. The empty boxes on the toolkit invited participants to write in additional academic stressors.

What are the intensity and frequency levels of different stressors?

Based on the completed Toolkit C - Stress Intensity + Frequency, the graph below shows both how intense and how frequent different stressors are for the students. Students were also asked to rank the severity of the stressors using color-coding dot stickers.

4

What are the most popular coping strategies that students consider to be effective? Not effective? Interested in trying?

Toolkit D asks students how they cope with stress. A packet of pre-printed labels with some common strategies was provided. Students were also asked to write-in their own coping strategies. This activity intends to understand the effectiveness of coping strategies that help college students to deal with stress, as well as gather more coping strategies.
